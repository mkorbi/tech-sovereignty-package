# Strengthening Europe's Tech Sovereignty — Management Summary

*Source: European Commission, "Strengthening Europe's Tech Sovereignty" (Shaping Europe's digital future). Page last updated 19 May 2026. Research compiled June 2026.*

## What tech sovereignty means

Tech sovereignty is Europe's ability to act independently in the digital world by developing and controlling key technologies, data, and infrastructure, while reducing reliance on non-EU providers. As underlined by Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in the 2025 State of the Union, it is treated as central to European competitiveness, resilience, and strategic autonomy.

The driving concern is dependency: the EU currently relies on non-EU countries for over 80% of key digital products, services, infrastructure, and intellectual property. The policy aims to reduce that exposure to strengthen economic strength, security, and long-term competitiveness.

The Commission frames five objectives for tech sovereignty: strengthening competitiveness, resilience and security; ensuring strategic autonomy in key digital technologies; supporting open and fair digital markets; protecting citizens' rights and democratic processes online; and enabling innovation and long-term technological leadership.

## How the strategy is structured

Tech sovereignty is not a single law but an umbrella over roughly seventeen initiatives, spanning new investment programmes, strategies, and regulations. They fall into six thematic clusters:

**Artificial intelligence** — the AI Continent Action Plan and its Apply AI Strategy, the AI Factories compute-infrastructure programme, and the AI Act (the first comprehensive legal framework for AI).

**The Digital Decade** — the overarching 2030 target-setting framework for Europe's digital transformation across infrastructure, skills, business, and public services.

**Infrastructure and strategic technologies** — the European Chips Act (semiconductors), the Quantum Europe Strategy, and the Digital Networks Act (connectivity rules).

**Data and innovation** — the Data Union Strategy, the Data Act, the EU Startup and Scaleup Strategy, the Digital Omnibus (simplification), the EU Open Source Strategy (the software-layer pillar promoting European open alternatives across the stack), and the EU Open-Source Solutions Catalogue.

**Cloud sovereignty** — the Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA), the legislative anchor that builds EU data-centre/compute capacity and defines four sovereignty assurance levels, together with the Cloud Sovereignty Framework (SEAL), the Commission's practical scoring tool already used to procure sovereign cloud for the EU institutions. This cluster is the part of the agenda most directly aimed at the ~€80% dependency on non-EU cloud providers.

**A fair, competitive and secure digital environment** — the Digital Services Act and the Digital Markets Act, governing online platforms and large "gatekeeper" platforms.

**Cyber and IT security** — the Cyber Resilience Act and the ICT Supply Chain Security Toolbox.

## The two policy modes

The initiatives use two complementary levers. The first is **building European capacity** through investment and coordination: tens of billions of euros are being channelled into compute (e.g. €20 billion InvestAI facility for up to five AI Gigafactories; ~€10 billion in EuroHPC supercomputing and AI Factories over 2021–2027), semiconductor manufacturing (over €31.5 billion of public and private investment approved across seven first-of-a-kind facilities), quantum, and connectivity.

The second is **regulation that shapes the market** — the AI Act, DSA, DMA, Data Act, and Cyber Resilience Act set rules that protect rights, ensure fair competition, and embed security. A notable recent shift is toward *simplification*: the Digital Omnibus (Nov 2025) and Digital Networks Act (Jan 2026) consolidate and streamline existing rules to cut compliance costs, reflecting recommendations from the Draghi and Letta reports on European competitiveness.

## Status snapshot

The portfolio mixes mature, in-force regulation with newer strategies and proposals still moving through the legislative process. Already in force: the AI Act, Chips Act (since Sept 2023), DSA, DMA, Cyber Resilience Act, and Data Act. Recently adopted or proposed: the Digital Networks Act (adopted as a proposal 21 Jan 2026), the Digital Omnibus (proposed Nov 2025), the Data Union Strategy, the Apply AI Strategy, and the Quantum Europe Strategy (2025). Investment programmes such as AI Factories are scaling rapidly — 19 AI Factories and 13 antennas were operational by late 2025, with at least nine new AI-optimised supercomputers planned.

## Why it matters

Taken together, these initiatives represent the EU's attempt to close an 80%+ dependency gap on a compressed timeline, balancing three sometimes-competing aims: catching up on capacity (compute, chips, data, networks), keeping markets fair and rights protected, and reducing the regulatory burden that critics argue has held back European scale-ups.
