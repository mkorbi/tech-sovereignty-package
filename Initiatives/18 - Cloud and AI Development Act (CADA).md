# Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA)

**Cluster:** Cloud sovereignty (data & infrastructure)
**Type:** Proposed Regulation — part of the AI Continent Action Plan and the Tech Sovereignty package
**Status:** Proposal adopted by the Commission on 3 June 2026

## Overview

The Cloud and AI Development Act will strengthen Europe's sovereignty and competitiveness in the cloud and AI ecosystem. It responds to two linked problems: Europe cannot currently build data-centre, cloud and compute capacity fast enough to meet AI demand (long permitting, limited access to energy, land and financing), and over-reliance on non-EU cloud providers is a risk to digital autonomy and resilience. CADA aims to make it faster to deploy sustainable cloud/data-centre infrastructure and to accelerate cloud and AI uptake in critical sectors, while keeping the market open to trusted partners.

## Three areas of action

**Research, development & innovation** — support next-generation cloud and AI (frontier, industrial and physical AI); launch "grand challenges"; drive adoption in strategic industrial and public sectors via national cloud/AI strategies and the Experience and Acceleration Centres for AI.

**Capacity** — at least **triple the EU's data-centre capacity within 5–7 years**; simplify and accelerate data-centre permitting; improve access to energy, land, water and financing; secure enough compute for AI, cloud and data-intensive applications.

**Autonomy** — introduce a **single EU-wide sovereignty framework** to assess cloud and AI sovereignty; accelerate sovereign cloud/AI for critical sectors while keeping most of the market open; reward EU added value and supply-chain resilience; establish a **common EU-level procurement framework** so public administrations pool purchasing power; and promote open-source solutions for resilience.

## The four sovereignty assurance levels

CADA defines cloud and AI sovereignty in four levels, used by public bodies according to their risk assessment (providers are recognised by Member States after an audit):

- **Level 1** — data is processed and stored in infrastructure located in the Union.
- **Level 2** — providers demonstrate independence from third countries and transparency over their software supply chain.
- **Level 3** — providers are owned and controlled from the EU and meet additional criteria such as personnel citizenship (the Commission may recognise third-country providers).
- **Level 4** — providers have full transparency and control over their software supply chain and no interference from a third country.

## How it supports tech sovereignty

CADA is the **legislative anchor for cloud and AI sovereignty**. It attacks the dependency problem on two fronts at once: it builds the *physical capacity* Europe lacks (data centres, compute, energy access) so European AI has somewhere to run, and it codifies *graduated sovereignty levels* plus joint public procurement so that sensitive workloads can be required to run on EU-controlled infrastructure. It operationalises the cloud pillar of the AI Continent Action Plan, depends on the compute built by AI Factories, draws on the data made available by the Data Union Strategy, and embeds an open-source-first approach that links to the EU Open-Source Solutions Catalogue.

## Relationship to the Cloud Sovereignty Framework (SEAL)

CADA's four legal assurance levels are the codified, EU-wide version of the assessment logic the Commission already piloted in procurement through the **Cloud Sovereignty Framework / SEAL** (see file 19). SEAL is the practical scoring tool used today; CADA turns that approach into law.
