# Cloud Sovereignty Framework (SEAL)

**Cluster:** Cloud sovereignty (assessment & procurement tool)
**Type:** Assessment methodology used in EU public procurement (not legislation)
**Status:** In use — applied in the 2025–26 EU institutions' sovereign-cloud tender; detailed guidance published 1 June 2026

## Overview

The Cloud Sovereignty Framework is the European Commission's practical tool for measuring how sovereign a cloud service really is. It was created for the procurement of sovereign cloud for the EU's own institutions and is now offered as a benchmark for any public or private organisation. In April 2026 the Commission awarded a **€180 million sovereign-cloud contract to four providers** under the Cloud III Dynamic Purchasing System, using this framework to assess bids — eligibility required at least **SEAL-2**.

## The two scores

The framework produces two complementary results:

**SEAL — Sovereignty Effectiveness Assurance Level.** A threshold-based rating of whether a provider meets defined levels of sovereignty and resilience. The named thresholds correspond to **SEAL-2 (data sovereignty)**, **SEAL-3 (technological autonomy)** and **SEAL-4 (full sovereignty)**. Lower rungs (commonly described as SEAL-0/SEAL-1) reflect little or no effective sovereign control — e.g. a service where EU contracts exist on paper but a foreign parent could still compel access.

**Overall sovereignty score.** A weighted score built from **48 specific criteria**, each with a defined scoring methodology, grouped into **eight categories**: strategic; legal and jurisdictional; data and AI; operational; supply chain; technological; security and compliance; and environmental sustainability. Supply chain is among the most heavily weighted dimensions.

## How it supports tech sovereignty

SEAL turns "sovereignty" from a marketing claim into a **measurable, auditable score** — and then uses the EU's own purchasing power to demand it. By requiring SEAL-2 for its institutions' cloud, the Commission "leads by example," creating real market demand for EU-controlled cloud and giving public bodies a ready-made yardstick instead of negotiating sovereignty case by case. It directly attacks the dependency that the whole tech-sovereignty agenda targets: control over where sensitive data and workloads run, and over the supply chain beneath them.

## Relationship to CADA and the wider agenda

SEAL is the **operational counterpart to the Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA, file 18)**: SEAL is the scoring tool already used in tenders, while CADA codifies a comparable four-level sovereignty framework into EU law for public-sector procurement. SEAL also complements security certification efforts such as the EU Cybersecurity Certification Scheme for Cloud Services (EUCS) and the Cyber Resilience Act, and reinforces the data-sovereignty goals of the Data Union Strategy.
